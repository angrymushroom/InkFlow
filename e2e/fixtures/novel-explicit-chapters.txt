The Clockwork Inheritance
A Novel

Chapter I

The letter arrived on a Tuesday, which Mira Ashford had always considered the most unremarkable day of the week. She was standing at the kitchen window, watching rain dissolve the last of the garden's autumn colors, when the postman pushed the envelope under the door.

She did not recognise the handwriting. The script was angular and deliberate, the ink a deep burgundy rather than ordinary black. Her name was spelled correctly, which was more than most people managed with Ashford.

Inside was a single folded sheet and a brass key that clattered onto the flagstone floor when she shook the envelope. The sheet read: "The workshop at 14 Vernier Lane is yours now. Destroy nothing. Tell no one. — E."

E. She had no relative, no friend, no acquaintance whose name began with E.

She picked up the key. It was heavier than it looked, engraved along its shaft with small interlocking gears. When she tilted it toward the window the gears seemed, for a fraction of a second, to move.

Her neighbour Callum Bright knocked at half past nine, as he did every Tuesday, to return whichever tool he had borrowed the week before. This time it was a spirit level.

"You look like you've seen a ghost," he said, handing it over.

"I've inherited a workshop," she said.

He waited for the rest of the sentence. It did not come.

"Right," he said at last. "Tea?"

Chapter II

Vernier Lane was not on any map Mira owned, but it appeared readily enough on her phone between a glazier's and a shuttered accountancy firm. The building at number fourteen had a green door, a single dusty window at street level, and no nameplate.

The brass key turned easily.

Inside, the smell hit her first — machine oil, cedar shavings, and something faintly electrical, like the air before lightning. Workbenches lined three walls, each crowded with components she could not immediately name. Coils of copper wire. Glass cylinders holding liquids of improbable colors. Dozens of small brass mechanisms, some no larger than a walnut, each in a different state of assembly.

Along the back wall, on a shelf at eye height, sat a row of notebooks. Their spines were dated. The earliest read 1987; the most recent, last March.

Mira pulled the nearest one down and opened it at random.

The handwriting was the same burgundy ink as the letter. The page showed a diagram — a sphere enclosing a spiral, enclosing a smaller sphere — with annotations in a language she did not recognise. Beneath the diagram, in English, a single line: "Third iteration. The resonance holds."

She was still reading when the small brass mechanism on the bench to her left began, without any visible cause, to tick.

She did not run. She considered it, but she did not run.

Callum would have run, she thought. Callum was sensible.

She pulled up a stool and kept reading.

Chapter III

She returned every day that week. By Friday she had worked through four notebooks and established three facts. First: E stood for Elara, a woman who had occupied this workshop for at least thirty-five years. Second: Elara had been building something — the notebooks called it "the Resonance Engine" — that was apparently designed to translate mechanical motion into something the notes described only as "stored intention." Third: Elara had known Mira's name since before Mira was born.

That third fact was the one Mira kept circling.

On Saturday, Callum followed her to Vernier Lane despite her instructions to the contrary. He stood in the doorway for a long time, looking at the benches and the notebooks and the softly ticking mechanism, which had by now been joined by two others.

"This is not normal," he said.

"No," Mira agreed.

"Are you going to tell me what it is?"

"I don't know yet."

He picked up one of the smaller brass components, turned it over in his hands, and set it down with the exaggerated care of a man who suspects everything in the room might be explosive.

"I'll make tea," he said. He had found a small electric kettle on the far bench, plugged into an extension lead of impressive length. "And then you're going to explain what 'stored intention' means, because I read it over your shoulder just now and I have questions."

Mira almost smiled. "So do I," she said.
