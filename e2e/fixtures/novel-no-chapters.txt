These Hollow Walls
A Story Without Chapters

The room they gave me is at the top of the house. Soren says the altitude will help. He says a great many things with such confidence that I have begun to catalogue them, the way a naturalist catalogues beetles, without judgment, simply for the record.

He is not unkind. I want to be clear about that. He brings me broth in the evenings and sits at the desk by the window while I drink it, reading his papers in silence. The silence is a consideration. He knows I cannot tolerate noise while my head is like this.

The room has one unusual feature: the plaster on the north wall has been painted over so many times that the layers have begun to crack. In the cracks there are shapes. I do not think they were put there deliberately. Probably they are just cracks.

I am writing this to have something to do with my hands.

***

A week has passed, I think. The days are difficult to count because the light changes so slowly up here. Soren has gone to the city twice. He does not tell me what business he has there and I have learned not to ask.

My sister Fen visited yesterday and sat on the edge of the bed and held my hands and asked how I was managing, and I said fine, which is the word people want. She brought oranges. I have been eating them one segment at a time, slowly, because they are the most real-tasting things in the room.

The shapes in the plaster are more numerous than I first thought. I count them in the mornings when the light comes from the east and hits the wall at a low angle. There are at least eleven distinct forms. Some look like letters. One, near the cornice, looks like a door, which is either meaningful or not, depending on one's disposition.

I have a disposition, Soren says, toward excessive interpretation.

Perhaps.

***

I dreamed last night that the plaster was peeling away in long curling strips and behind it was not more plaster but a kind of darkness that was also a kind of space — not frightening, just immense, the way the sky is immense when you stand in a field and forget to be afraid of it.

In the dream I walked into it.

When I woke up Soren was asleep in the chair by the window with his papers still in his lap. He had not left me. I noted this. It went into the catalogue under a heading I do not have a word for yet.

Fen came again in the afternoon. She says my colour is better. She means well.

***

I have been looking at the crack near the cornice for eleven days. Today I noticed something I had missed before. The shape that looks like a door has, at its base, a shape that could be interpreted as a step.

One step. Leading up to the door.

I moved my chair to get a better angle and sat there for a long time, long enough that when Soren came in with the broth he stopped in the doorway and looked at me and then at the wall and then back at me without speaking.

"Do you see it?" I asked.

He came and stood behind me and looked.

"I see cracks in the plaster," he said, finally.

"Yes," I said.

There was a pause.

"What do you see?" he asked. His voice was careful. It is a voice he uses when he is being a doctor rather than a husband, and I have learned to hear the difference.

"The same thing," I said. "Cracks in the plaster."

He put his hand briefly on my shoulder. He went back downstairs. I kept looking.

The step is definitely there.

***

Fen says I should come to stay with her for a while once the weather turns. She has a spare room, ordinary walls, a cat that sleeps on the bed and is said to be very soothing. I told her I would think about it.

I am not ready to leave yet. I am not sure what I am waiting for.

The cracks are changing. Not dramatically. Just slightly, incrementally, the way a river changes its banks over a season. I can see it because I have been drawing them each morning in the back of this notebook, small precise sketches, dated. They are changing.

I showed the sketches to Soren. He looked at each one carefully and said that plaster does shift with temperature and humidity, which is true.

"But do you think that accounts for all of it?" I asked.

He looked at me for a moment in that careful way.

"I think rest is important," he said.

That is not an answer to the question I asked. I noted it, filed it.

***

This morning I woke before the light and sat in the dark and waited. When dawn came and hit the wall I saw it clearly for the first time: the door shape is complete now. Two upright lines, a horizontal at the top, the step at the base. And in the exact center of the door, at a height that would correspond to a handle, there is a small circular mark that was not there a week ago. I am certain of this because I have the sketches.

I sat for a long time.

Then I went to the wall and I pressed my palm flat against the plaster, over the circle, and held it there.

Nothing happened. Of course nothing happened.

But the plaster was warm.

I stood there with my palm on the warm plaster and outside the window the light was coming up gray and ordinary over the rooftops and inside the room everything was completely still.

I am not better yet. But I am beginning to think that better is not quite the right word for what is happening to me in this room.
