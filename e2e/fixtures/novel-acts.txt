THE LAST INHERITANCE
A Play in Three Acts

CHARACTERS:
VIVIENNE CROSS, 40s, a solicitor
DOMINIC HALE, 50s, her estranged uncle
PETRA, 30s, Dominic's assistant
YOUNG VIVIENNE, Vivienne at age 12 (appears in memory sequences)

---

ACT I

SCENE ONE

(A solicitor's office. Late afternoon. Filing cabinets, a large desk, two chairs. VIVIENNE sits behind the desk reviewing papers. A knock.)

VIVIENNE: Come in.

(PETRA enters, carrying a manila envelope.)

PETRA: A courier just brought this. Hand delivery. He wouldn't wait for a signature.

VIVIENNE: (not looking up) Who sent it?

PETRA: No return address. But the handwriting on the front— (she hesitates) I think you should look.

(VIVIENNE looks up. She takes the envelope. Studies the handwriting for a long moment. Sets it down without opening it.)

VIVIENNE: Cancel my four o'clock.

PETRA: It's Mr. Aldren. He's already on his way from—

VIVIENNE: Cancel it. Tell him I'll call tomorrow.

(PETRA exits. VIVIENNE sits alone with the envelope. She does not touch it. Lights shift to memory.)

SCENE TWO

(The same desk, but transformed — smaller, cluttered with a child's drawings. YOUNG VIVIENNE sits doing homework. DOMINIC enters, younger, a large coat still on.)

DOMINIC: Still up?

YOUNG VIVIENNE: You said you'd be home for dinner.

DOMINIC: I said I might be. There's a difference.

YOUNG VIVIENNE: Mum noticed.

DOMINIC: (sitting across from her) Your mother always notices. It's her gift and my misfortune. (beat) What are you working on?

YOUNG VIVIENNE: Fractions. I hate them.

DOMINIC: Everyone hates fractions until they realise the world runs on them. A half of this, a third of that. (He picks up one of the drawings.) This is good.

YOUNG VIVIENNE: It's the house.

DOMINIC: I can see that. Why is there a door in the roof?

YOUNG VIVIENNE: Because that's where the best room is.

(He looks at her. Faint smile.)

DOMINIC: Of course it is.

(Lights shift back to present.)

SCENE THREE

(VIVIENNE still at the desk. She opens the envelope. Inside: a single key and a handwritten note. She reads it. Her expression does not change, but something behind it does. She folds the note, pockets it, holds the key.)

PETRA: (entering) I've rescheduled Mr. Aldren. He was— (she reads VIVIENNE's stillness) What's happened?

VIVIENNE: My uncle is dead.

PETRA: Oh. I'm sorry. Were you close?

VIVIENNE: (a pause that contains an entire history) No.

(She stands. Picks up her coat.)

VIVIENNE: I'll be out for the rest of the day. If anyone asks, say I'm in conference.

PETRA: Where will you be?

VIVIENNE: I'm not entirely sure yet.

(She exits. PETRA stands a moment, then picks up the envelope. It's empty. She sets it back down.)

END OF ACT I

---

ACT II

SCENE ONE

(A storage unit on the edge of town. Fluorescent light. Metal shelving. VIVIENNE enters with a torch, finds a light switch. The unit is full of boxes, all labelled in the same handwriting as the envelope.)

VIVIENNE: (quietly) You kept everything.

(She moves along the shelves. Touches a box labelled LEGAL — HOUSE. Another labelled V's things, do not open.)

(She opens it.)

(Inside: the drawings from the memory sequence. The one with the door in the roof is on top.)

DOMINIC: (V.O., pre-recorded, playing from a small device in the box) If you're hearing this, you found the unit. I knew you would — you always understood keys better than doors. I have something to tell you, Vivienne, that I should have told you twenty years ago. Your mother asked me not to. She's gone now and I'm going and so that leaves only you to decide what to do with it.

(She sits on a box. The recording continues.)

DOMINIC: (V.O.) The house was never ours to sell. It was never ours at all. It belongs to —

(The recording distorts, skips. Silence. Then static.)

VIVIENNE: (to the empty room) Of course.

(She looks at the key again. Then at the box of drawings. She takes the one of the house with the door in the roof. Folds it carefully. Puts it in her pocket with the note.)

SCENE TWO

(Outside the storage unit. PETRA is waiting by a car.)

VIVIENNE: I told you to go home.

PETRA: You said you weren't sure where you were going. It seemed prudent.

VIVIENNE: How did you know I'd come here?

PETRA: (small pause) Your uncle called the office. Three months ago. He said if anything happened to him, to make sure you found the unit key before the estate solicitors did. He mailed it to me directly.

(Beat.)

VIVIENNE: You've had it for three months.

PETRA: He asked me to wait until you were ready.

VIVIENNE: Ready.

PETRA: His word.

VIVIENNE: How would you know if I was ready?

PETRA: (simply) I work for you. I know when you're not.

(A long pause.)

VIVIENNE: There's a house. I need to find it.

PETRA: I know. I looked it up when he called. It's about forty minutes from here.

(She holds out her car keys.)

VIVIENNE: (taking them) You're irritatingly competent.

PETRA: I know that too.

(They get in the car.)

END OF ACT II

---

ACT III

SCENE ONE

(A house. Empty, but not derelict — maintained, as if expecting occupants. VIVIENNE and PETRA move through it with torches. They reach the staircase.)

VIVIENNE: (looking up) There.

(At the top: a door in a low roof space. The key fits.)

(Inside: a small room, slanted walls, a single window with a view over fields. A chair. A desk. On the desk, a thick file.)

VIVIENNE: (reading the cover) Cross v. Hale. 1987.

PETRA: What happened in 1987?

VIVIENNE: I was seven. (beat) My parents argued about money all winter. I remember because they'd stop whenever I came in the room. I thought it was about me.

(She opens the file.)

VIVIENNE: It wasn't about me.

SCENE TWO

(Later. Both women sitting on the floor with papers between them. The torch propped against the desk.)

PETRA: So he paid the debt. Quietly. And never told anyone.

VIVIENNE: He let my father believe he'd solved it himself. For twenty years.

PETRA: Why?

VIVIENNE: (a long pause) Because my father would have refused the help. He was that kind of proud.

PETRA: And your uncle?

VIVIENNE: (quietly) He was the other kind. (beat) The kind who'd rather be resented than watch someone drown.

(She looks at the drawing in her hand — the house with the door in the roof.)

VIVIENNE: I drew this the week we nearly lost the house. I didn't know that's what it was. I just knew the roof felt like the safest part.

PETRA: What will you do with all this?

VIVIENNE: (standing) Settle it. Properly. Whatever that takes.

(She moves to the window. Dawn is beginning outside.)

VIVIENNE: He left me the house. That means he left me the question of what it's worth.

PETRA: And what is it worth?

VIVIENNE: (looking at the view) That's the first question I don't have a legal answer for.

(Lights fade slowly.)

END OF ACT III

END OF PLAY
